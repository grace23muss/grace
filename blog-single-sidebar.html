Executive Overview

I built a rating model for ten leased Dollarama stores across Ontario, each occupying approximately 10,000 sq ft of joisted‑masonry retail space. After location‑specific credits and surcharges, the portfolio produces CA $98.8 K in written premium and a blended expected loss ratio of 56 %, comfortably below the corporate hurdle of 65 %. Every individual store meets that hurdle; Sudbury and Thunder Bay clear it by the widest margin because they are priced more conservatively to reflect weaker fire protection.

Scope and Assumptions

The analysis covers tenant improvements, stock and equipment, and a twelve month business‑income limit of CA $1.2 million per location. Liability limits are CA $2 million per occurrence and CA $4 million aggregate. All stores fall under ISO Mercantile Class 2 and have reported no significant losses during the past five years aside from a single CA $2 000 slip‑and‑fall claim that has closed. Replacement‑cost values average CA $1.3 million per location.

Rating Methodology

Property is rated from an ISO mercantile base of 0.50 %–0.60 % of TIV. Credits of ten per‑cent are applied for wet‑pipe sprinklers, for central‑station alarms, and for moderate building age. Where protection is weaker, a theft or non‑sprinkler surcharge of five to ten per‑cent is added. General liability is rated at CA $1.25–CA $1.35 per CA $1 000 of sales and receives a 15–20 % clean‑history credit. A 10 % package discount links the two lines, after which a three‑per‑cent Ontario premium tax and a CA $50 policy fee are added. Loss cost is assumed at 0.25 % of TIV, the long‑run industry average for low‑hazard mercantile property.

Portfolio Results

Total property premium is CA $56 653; liability premium is CA $42 132; combined package premium is CA $98 785. Expected loss dollars are CA $55 519, yielding a blended expected loss ratio of 56.2 %. Ottawa sits exactly at the 65 % threshold, while Sudbury, the most conservatively priced due to absent sprinklers, stands at 41.7 %.

Observations

Rate discipline plus standard credits generate roughly nine points of margin between expected and target loss ratio, leaving room for catastrophe or crime loadings without eroding profitability. The model architecture is uniform; adding a new store requires cloning an Inputs sheet and typing the city name in the dashboard.

Recommendations

We recommend three incremental actions. First, apply a three basis point hail surcharge to the Windsor and London stores, both situated in Ontario’s hail corridor, which will add roughly CA $500 in property revenue per location. Second, extend a 15 percent sprinkler retrofit credit to the Sudbury store once the landlord installs a compliant wet pipe system, keeping that location’s expected loss ratio near 50 percent. Third, attach a CA $50 000 Crime (employee dishonesty) endorsement across all stores, an enhancement that still leaves the blended portfolio loss ratio comfortably below 60 percent.

Conclusion

In summary, we analyzed ten leased Dollarama stores across Ontario and, after applying COPE-driven rating credits and surcharges, produced a book that generates CA $ 98,800 in premium at a blended expected loss ratio of 56 percent. Our scope covered replacement‑cost tenant improvements, stock, twelve‑month business‑income limits, and CA $2 million/CA $4 million liability limits, all backed by five clean loss years. Rates derived from ISO Mercantile Class 2, adjusted for sprinklers, alarms, age, and theft exposure, then packaged and taxed per Ontario rules. The resulting portfolio meets or beats the 65 percent profitability hurdle at every location, with Sudbury and Thunder Bay demonstrating the margin created by stricter protection loadings. Observations confirm nine points of headroom for CAT or crime enhancements, and we outline targeted actions hail surcharge, sprinkler retrofit credit, and crime endorsement to fortify both revenue and risk control. Overall, the model is defensible, scalable, and immediately deployable for additional mercantile schedules.
